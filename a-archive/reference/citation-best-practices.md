# Citation Best Practices: When and How to Cite

> **Scope:** This guide summarizes broadly applicable practices drawn from APA, MLA, Chicago, and Purdue OWL guidance. Exact punctuation, ordering, capitalization, and formatting vary by citation style, discipline, instructor, journal, and publisher.

## The core principle

Cite a source whenever your writing depends on **someone else’s words, ideas, evidence, creative work, or distinctive intellectual contribution**. A citation should make it clear:

1. **what** material came from a source;
2. **which** source supplied it; and
3. **where** in that source the material can be found, when a locator is available.

APA describes citation as a way to document non-common facts and figures and recommends using primary sources where possible.[^1] MLA similarly treats an in-text citation as a concise pointer to the corresponding works-cited entry and, when relevant, to a specific location in the source.[^2]


## Choose the citation system before drafting

The underlying principles of attribution are stable, but the mechanics differ by style. Follow the system required by the assignment, department, journal, or publisher; local instructions override a general handbook.

| Style | Citation in the text | End matter | Common context |
|---|---|---|---|
| **APA** | Author–date, such as `(Nguyen, 2025)` | **References** | Social and behavioural sciences and many professional fields |
| **MLA** | Usually author–page, such as `(Nguyen 42)` | **Works Cited** | Humanities and text-centred analysis |
| **Chicago notes and bibliography** | Superscript note numbers linked to footnotes or endnotes | **Bibliography** | Humanities and source-rich historical writing |
| **Chicago author–date** | Author–date, often with a page number | **Reference List** | Sciences and social sciences |
| **IEEE** | Consecutive numbers in square brackets, such as `[4]` | **References**, ordered by first appearance | Engineering and technical writing |

APA, MLA, Chicago, and IEEE all connect a brief citation at the point of use to fuller source information, but they organize author names, dates, locators, notes, and reference entries differently.[^18][^19][^20][^21]

## When you should cite

### 1. Direct quotations

Always cite words copied from a source, even when the quotation is only a few words long. Use quotation marks or block-quotation formatting as required by the selected style. Include a page number, paragraph number, timestamp, section, line number, or another usable locator when one exists.[^2][^3]

**Example**

> The authors describe citation as an essential part of scholarly accountability (Author, Year, p. 14).

Do not make up page or paragraph numbers. For an unpaginated source, use another locator permitted by the style, such as a heading, paragraph number, timestamp, chapter, or section.[^3][^4]

### 2. Paraphrases

Cite an idea taken from a source even when you have completely rewritten it in your own words. Replacing words with synonyms, changing sentence order, or condensing the passage does not make the underlying idea yours.[^5][^6]

A good paraphrase should both:

- represent the source accurately; and
- be substantially expressed in your own language and sentence structure.

A citation alone does not cure a deficient paraphrase. If you merely rearrange the source’s words, delete a phrase or two, or swap in synonyms while keeping the original sentence structure, the passage remains plagiarism even with the source credited; check your paraphrase against the original’s sentence and paragraph structure, since copying that structure is itself a form of plagiarism.[^6][^17]

### 3. Summaries

Cite summaries of an article, book, report, argument, theory, or body of evidence. A summary may cover an entire work rather than a single passage, but the intellectual content still comes from the source.[^6]

### 4. Facts, statistics, and data that are not common knowledge

Cite numerical results, survey findings, measurements, dates that are not widely known, technical claims, and other externally verifiable information.[^1]

**Usually cite:**

- “The study reported a 17% increase.”
- “The intervention was tested with 438 participants.”
- “The regulation came into force on a particular date.”
- “The dataset contains 2.4 million records.”

### 5. Theories, definitions, models, methods, and specialized terminology

Cite the source that introduced, defined, or substantially developed a theory, model, framework, measure, algorithm, research method, or specialized concept. A standard term may eventually become common knowledge within a field, but its distinctive definition or formulation may still require attribution.

### 6. Another person’s interpretation or argument

Cite conclusions, explanations, interpretations, criticisms, hypotheses, and argumentative structures developed by another author. This applies even when you agree with the source or build on it rather than quote it.

### 7. Images, figures, tables, graphs, maps, media, and datasets

Cite material that you reproduce, adapt, redraw, translate, or use as the basis for a new visual. Citation does **not** replace the need to obtain permission when copyright or licence terms require it.

Label adapted material clearly—for example, “Adapted from …”—when the relevant style or licence requires that wording.

### 8. Code, software, instruments, scales, and research materials

Cite software, packages, code repositories, questionnaires, assessment instruments, laboratory protocols, and other tools when they materially contributed to the work. Follow discipline-specific reporting standards in addition to the general citation style.

### 9. Your own previously published or submitted work

Cite your earlier work when reusing its wording, data, arguments, figures, or results. Being the original author does not make undisclosed reuse acceptable in every academic or publishing context. MLA advises authors to cite their own work as they would cite another source.[^7]

### 10. Personal communications and non-recoverable sources

Interviews, private emails, conversations, classroom remarks, and similar sources may require an in-text citation or textual attribution but no reference-list entry because readers cannot retrieve them. APA, for example, treats personal communications this way.[^8] Obtain consent before identifying a private individual or sharing sensitive information.

### 11. Generative-AI output, when required

Disclose or cite generative-AI use when required by the assignment, institution, publisher, funding body, or applicable style guide. Do not treat an AI-generated citation as evidence that a source exists. Consult and cite the original source whenever possible; MLA specifically recommends opening and evaluating sources linked by AI systems rather than relying on the AI summary alone.[^9]

## When a citation is usually unnecessary

### Common knowledge

A citation is generally unnecessary for information that is widely known and easily verified by the intended audience, such as:

- Ottawa is the capital of Canada.
- Water freezes at 0 °C under standard atmospheric pressure.
- The Second World War ended in 1945.

“Common knowledge” is context-dependent. A fact may be common knowledge for specialists but unfamiliar to a general audience. When uncertain, cite—writing-centre guidance treats "cite if in doubt" as the safe default.[^6]

### Your original contribution

You do not cite your own new analysis, argument, synthesis, calculation, or conclusion—provided it is genuinely yours and clearly distinguished from sourced material.

### Personal experience or observation

Your own experience ordinarily does not require citation, although research involving participants, field observations, clinical records, or confidential information may require ethical approval, methodological documentation, or anonymization.

## How to cite well

### 1. Cite at the point of use

Place the citation close enough to the borrowed material that the reader can tell exactly which clause or sentence it supports. Avoid placing a single citation at the end of a long paragraph when the paragraph contains claims from several sources or mixes sourced material with your own analysis.

Signal phrases can improve clarity:

- “Nguyen (2025) argues that …”
- “According to the commission’s report …”
- “The survey found that …”

### 2. Include precise locators

For a quotation or a claim tied to a specific passage, give the most precise stable locator available. MLA says location markers such as page numbers, line numbers, or timestamps should be included when relevant.[^2] Chicago’s examples likewise use specific pages in notes or in-text citations rather than merely listing the full page range of an article.[^10]

### 3. Prefer primary sources

Read and cite the original study, judgement, dataset, speech, archival document, standard, or policy whenever reasonably possible. APA recommends citing primary sources and using secondary citations sparingly.[^1]

When the original source is unavailable, make the indirect relationship explicit according to the selected style. Do not imply that you personally consulted a source you did not read.

### 4. Cite the version you actually used

Record the correct edition, translation, release, database version, webpage version, or publication format. Page numbers and content can differ between editions. Citation generators cannot reliably determine whether you selected the correct version.[^11]

### 5. Match every in-text citation to the reference list

In systems that use a reference list or works-cited list, each in-text citation should lead to a full entry, and each listed entry should normally be cited in the text. APA states this correspondence explicitly.[^12] Exceptions can include personal communications and other source types handled only in text.

### 6. Avoid both undercitation and overcitation

**Undercitation** obscures where borrowed material begins or ends.  
**Overcitation** can make prose difficult to read and may unnecessarily repeat the same citation in every sentence.

When several consecutive sentences clearly paraphrase one source, introduce the source at the beginning and cite again only when needed to prevent ambiguity, when another source intervenes, or when the paragraph changes focus. APA specifically cautions against repeating the same citation in every sentence when the source and topic have not changed.[^13]

### 7. Use one style consistently

Choose the style required by the instructor, journal, publisher, or discipline. Chicago offers both notes-and-bibliography and author-date systems and advises writers to follow the system customary in their field or required by the publisher or instructor.[^14]

Do not combine APA in-text citations with an MLA works-cited list or switch punctuation conventions midway through a document.

### 8. Use stable identifiers and direct links

For online scholarly sources, prefer a DOI or other persistent identifier when the style permits it. Link as directly as possible to the item being cited rather than to a search-results page, university proxy, or unrelated homepage.[^10]

### 9. Treat changing webpages carefully

Include a retrieval or access date when the applicable style calls for one—especially for unarchived content designed to change over time. APA generally reserves retrieval dates for changing, unarchived works.[^15] MLA treats access dates as optional but notes that they can be useful for undated, unstable, or easily altered material.[^16]

### 10. Check citation-generator output

Citation managers and generators are useful drafting tools, not final authorities. Verify:

- author names and order;
- publication date;
- title capitalization;
- source type;
- edition or version;
- volume, issue, and page range;
- DOI or URL;
- italics and punctuation; and
- whether the in-text citation fits the sentence.

Purdue OWL warns that generators depend on the information supplied and cannot exercise contextual judgement.[^11]

### 11. Keep research notes with source information attached

Record bibliographic details, page numbers, quotations, paraphrases, and your own comments while reading. Mark copied text immediately so it cannot later be mistaken for your own wording. Purdue recommends using citation habits during note-taking, not only when writing the final paper.[^17]

### 12. Verify that the source supports the claim

A citation should support the precise statement it follows. Do not cite a source merely because it is related to the general topic. Check the original context, methods, limitations, publication status, corrections, and—where relevant—whether the work has been retracted.

### 13. Separate citation from copyright and permission

A citation gives scholarly credit; it does not automatically grant permission to reproduce a copyrighted image, table, figure, substantial text extract, or other protected work. Check the applicable copyright law, licence, publisher policy, and institutional guidance separately. For Creative Commons material, follow the exact licence conditions and identify the title, creator, source, licence, and any changes when available.[^22][^23]

### 14. Cite datasets and software as research outputs

When a dataset or software package materially supports the work, cite the resource itself rather than only citing a paper that mentions it. Record enough information for another researcher to identify the exact object used:

- creator or project team;
- publication or release year;
- title;
- version or release number;
- repository, publisher, or distributor; and
- DOI, other persistent identifier, or stable URL.

Version information is especially important for software and changing datasets because different releases may produce different results.[^24]

### 15. Check publication and version status

Before relying on a source, confirm that you are using the intended version and that the work has not been corrected, withdrawn, or retracted. When a correction or newer version materially changes the claim, cite the corrected or updated record and explain the version choice when necessary. Crossmark is one mechanism publishers use to expose updates and current document status.[^25]

### 16. Distinguish recoverable from non-recoverable material

A reader should normally be able to retrieve anything listed in the references or works cited. Private emails, conversations, and some interviews are handled differently because they are not publicly recoverable. Public posts, archived webpages, repositories, and published transcripts can usually receive full entries. Apply the selected style’s rule rather than treating all digital communication alike.

## Special cases

### Secondary citations

Use a secondary citation only when the original source cannot reasonably be consulted. Make clear which source you actually read, and list only that recoverable source unless the selected style instructs otherwise. Never imply that you reviewed an original work you did not access.

### Preprints, accepted manuscripts, and versions of record

Identify the publication state accurately. A preprint may differ from the peer-reviewed version of record; an accepted manuscript may differ in pagination and copy-editing. Prefer the version of record when it is available and appropriate, but cite the version you actually used.

### Translations and editions

Name the translator and edition when those details affect wording, pagination, interpretation, or reproducibility. Do not copy a locator from a different edition unless you have verified it.

### Multiple sources supporting one claim

Cite each source that materially supports the claim, but avoid decorative citation lists. A group of citations should represent sources you have actually read and whose evidence you can distinguish.

### Generative AI and source discovery

Treat AI-generated summaries and bibliographies as leads, not evidence. Verify that each source exists, open it, read the relevant passage, and cite the original material. Separately disclose or cite the AI system when required by the applicable policy or style.[^9]

## A note on accidental plagiarism

Most plagiarism in student and professional work is not deliberate; it results from improper paraphrasing, summarizing, quoting, or citing rather than intent to deceive. Two habits prevent the common failures: while taking notes, mark every copied span immediately (a literal "Q" or quotation marks) so borrowed wording can never later be mistaken for your own, and put quotation marks around any distinctive phrase you keep verbatim even inside an otherwise reworded paraphrase. Not knowing the conventions is an explanation, not an excuse, so the safe move under uncertainty is always to attribute.[^17]

## A practical citation workflow

1. **Capture the source immediately.** Save complete metadata when you first encounter a source, preferably in a reference manager.
2. **Record the exact version.** Note the edition, release, dataset version, webpage date, DOI, and access date where relevant.
3. **Separate source text from your notes.** Mark verbatim text with quotation marks and preserve page numbers or other locators.
4. **Draft from understanding.** Close or set aside the source before paraphrasing, then compare your wording and structure with the original.
5. **Cite while drafting.** Insert citations at the point of use rather than leaving all documentation until the end.
6. **Audit every claim.** Confirm that the cited source actually supports the sentence and that the locator is correct.
7. **Reconcile citations and references.** Check that every recoverable in-text citation has an entry and that every listed work is used.
8. **Run a final status check.** Verify links, DOI resolution, versions, corrections, and retractions before submission.
9. **Inspect generated citations.** Reference managers such as Zotero can reduce clerical work, but their output still requires manual review.[^26]

## Quick decision guide

| Question | Cite? |
|---|---:|
| Did I copy the exact words? | **Yes** |
| Did I paraphrase or summarize someone’s idea? | **Yes** |
| Did I use a statistic, dataset, figure, image, or table? | **Yes** |
| Is this a specialized, debatable, or non-obvious factual claim? | **Usually yes** |
| Is this a theory, model, method, definition, or distinctive concept from another source? | **Yes** |
| Is this widely known and uncontested by the intended audience? | **Usually no** |
| Is this my own new analysis or conclusion? | **No**, unless it builds on identifiable sources that also need citation |
| Am I unsure whether the information is common knowledge? | **Cite it** |
| Did I find the claim through a secondary source or AI summary? | **Consult and cite the original source whenever possible** |
| Did a citation generator create the entry? | **Verify it before submission** |

## Final checklist

- [ ] Every quotation is visibly marked and cited.
- [ ] Every paraphrase and summary is cited, and reworded in structure as well as vocabulary.
- [ ] Non-common facts, statistics, and data have sources.
- [ ] Citations are placed beside the claims they support.
- [ ] Page numbers or other locators are included when appropriate.
- [ ] Primary sources are used where reasonably available.
- [ ] The exact edition or version consulted is identified.
- [ ] Every in-text citation matches a reference entry, subject to style-specific exceptions.
- [ ] Figures, tables, images, code, software, and datasets are credited.
- [ ] Citation-generator output has been checked manually.
- [ ] The same citation style is used consistently.
- [ ] Assignment, institutional, journal, and publisher requirements have been checked.
- [ ] Copyright and licence requirements have been checked separately from citation.
- [ ] Dataset and software versions are identified where reproducibility depends on them.
- [ ] Publication status, corrections, withdrawals, and retractions have been checked.
- [ ] AI-suggested sources have been opened and verified against the original material.
