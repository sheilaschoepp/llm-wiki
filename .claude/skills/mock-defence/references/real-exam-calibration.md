# Real-exam calibration — what a real candidacy exam asked

How one real candidacy exam actually ran, distilled from the candidate's session notes with every name and proposal specific abstracted away. Load it when designing any session (drill or question bank): it calibrates register, difficulty, and coverage against a real room rather than against interview-prep instinct. It complements, and is outranked by, a committee's own `asked-questions.md` (see SKILL.md Conventions): that file records what the user's actual committee asked and wins on specifics; this one carries the transferable shape. Instantiate every archetype from the live document — the bracketed slots are placeholders, not wording to reuse. One scope caveat: the names are abstracted but the exam's shape is not — this was an empirical-science candidacy, and archetypes about runs, statistical significance, testbeds, modalities, and formal core models carry that shape. The four-register structure transfers across fields; for a non-experimental discipline (a historical, qualitative, or theoretical exam), re-instantiate the archetypes as frames — replace the empirical question behind the bracket, not just the bracket.

## How the Real Room Ran

- Every member asked questions, supervisors included, each within a fixed time allotment; the captured questioning ran through the members in a fixed order, twice. (The supervisor's questions were not captured in the candidate's notes, so the seat patterns below do not describe them.)
- Fundamentals spot-checks came first, before anything about the document.
- Every captured questioner brought published work the document never cited.
- Members mixed positions and advice in with questions — stances the candidate had to engage, not queries to answer.
- The exam ended on logistics and the candidate's formation, not on the document.

## The Four Registers

**1. Fundamentals spot-checks.** Early, formal, from the field's shared canon. They test the candidate, not the document, and they do not track the asking member's own research specialty — a member whose research is far from the canon may still open with it. Archetypes:

- Are you familiar with [the adjacent field's formal framework]?
- Can you write down the formal definition of [the field's core model]?
- What about [the core model's more general extension]?
- How does [a core component of the model] change in [the setting the proposal actually works in]?
- How is [the adjacent framework's central quantity] related to [your field's]?
- What is the difference between [two standard experimental designs the methodology relies on]?

**2. Uncited-literature probes.** At least one per arms-length examiner — published work the document never cites, offered as a pointer with a question attached. One examiner said outright he had found papers the related-work coverage missed. The pointer shapes examiners actually use:

- A neighbouring community's formalism ("there is a rich literature in [community] on [their formalism] — how does your [design element] relate to that?").
- A named system from the recent literature ("does [system] do anything related to this?").
- A published negative result about the mechanism the proposal relies on ("[a prior study] found [the mechanism] drifted away from [the property you need] — how do you prevent that?").
- A practice or industry convention rather than a paper ("[a practice community] has [a convention] that solves part of this — would it be worth using?").
- Industry white papers naming alternative [architectures/designs], with an "is yours one of these?" question attached.

**3. Breadth beyond the document.** The roads not taken. Archetypes:

- A single [testbed] could be problematic. If you stick with [the chosen one], what would you be missing?
- What about [a testbed with a property the chosen one lacks]?
- What about [an applied domain] as a testbed? (Sometimes with the examiner voicing the disanalogy themselves.)
- How will [the input] be represented to [the system] — [modality A], [modality B], or something else?
- What is specific to [your approach] now that [the new paradigm] is in play? How is this different from [the prior paradigm]?
- What if you get [a load-bearing design choice] wrong — it works in one case and fails in another? How would you know, and how do you fix it partway through?
- [The designed artifact] might need to change over time — how do you fix it if it proves insufficient for the task?

**4. The logistics close.** Real exams end on the candidate's formation and plan, not on the document. Archetypes:

- What is your paper plan? What is the interdependence between the papers?
- Are you planning on graduating in [year] or [year + 1]? What venues are you thinking of?
- How many runs will you have — will you have statistical significance? How long will each experiment take?
- What meta-skills are you looking to learn? (Often with advice attached.)
- [The most exciting part of the plan] is also the most costly — what about [a cheaper substitute]?
- When will you be ready to defend — what does success look like?

## Seat Patterns

How the questioning divided across the room, by the seat's relation to the work. Use these to give each member a realistic arc when their profile or prep file is thin:

- **Related-area arms-length examiner** (their field neighbours the proposal's): opened with the formal canon (register 1), then pressed the proposal's design and representation choices; brought their own community's formalism as the uncited pointer; asked the lifecycle question (what if the design proves wrong or must evolve); the second round widened to alternative testbeds and closed on the paper plan and the candidate's future.
- **Near-field arms-length examiner** (same broad field, different subarea): feasibility and scope — which [systems] exactly, the deployment-time costs the development setting hides, the backup plan if [the core resource] disappoints, scale-down options, whether fixing [a design choice] is limiting, single-testbed risk; the second round went to run counts and significance, experiment duration, timeline, venues, and the "costly but exciting versus cheaper substitute" trade.
- **Senior best-aligned member** (closest to the work among non-supervisors): positioning and mechanism — what is genuinely new versus the prior paradigm, alternative [protocols/architectures] from practice and industry, who authors the rules the design assumes, whether [the studied mechanism's] cost belongs in the objective, how [roles/responsibilities] get assigned; closed on readiness and success criteria.
- **Supervisor**: questions like every other seat — the fixed time allotment includes the supervisor, so do not model the seat as silent. Its content was not captured in the candidate's notes; play its register per the general backstop doctrine (supportive framing and plan questions rather than attacks on the seams).

## Drill Lessons

- A drill that skips any of the four registers rehearses a narrower exam than the real one.
- Every member questions, supervisors included — a drill that leaves the supervisor seat silent rehearses a narrower room than the real one.
- Voice some turns as position statements the candidate must engage ("[the studied mechanism] has a cost — less of it is better"), not as questions.
- Run the table more than once: the second round is sharper or broader, never a repeat of the first.
- Research focus predicts a member's deep-dives, not their fundamentals checks — the formal opening can come from anyone.
- Members hand out advice and half-remembered pointers mid-exam; realistic drills do too, and the honesty rule holds — voice only real works or honest area-level pointers, and flag anything the candidate should verify before citing.
