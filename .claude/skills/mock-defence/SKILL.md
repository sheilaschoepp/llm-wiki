# mock-defence

Run a mock defence: role-play the user's committee from real profiles and grill them on the document they're defending, so the real exam holds no surprises.

## Purpose

This skill rehearses a candidacy or thesis defence. It reads the document under defence and the committee's profiles, then plays each examiner — their research lens, their standards, their questioning style — and presses the candidate through escalating questions until the weak answers surface and get fixed. The value is an honest, adversarial rehearsal grounded in *these* examiners and *this* document, not generic interview prep.

The artefact at `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/mock-defence-YYYY-MM-DD-HHMM-{event}.md` is the record of one session: what was asked, where answers held versus wobbled, and what to read or tighten before the real thing. The user owns the proposal; this skill stress-tests it.

This skill is **prep-only and wiki-orthogonal** — it serves the user's defence, not the wiki. It writes to `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/` and nowhere else, and touches no `1-wiki/` bookkeeping.

## Conventions (Recap)

- **The document under defence** and its event slug default to the values in `references/defaults.md` — a small user-maintained file, edited when the defended document changes. For any other document (a thesis, a paper talk), the user names it. Read it before questioning — for a PDF over 10 pages, read in chunks with the `pages:` parameter. Question from what the document actually says, not from memory of the field. The defaults file is authoritative for the default path; if a prep file cites the document under a different path, trust the defaults file, not the stale note.
- **Committees** are selectable. Each lives in its own folder under `references/committees/<name>/`, with a short `committee.md` manifest (one line per field: display name, kind, summary, where its questions seed from) and a `profiles/` folder of one profile per member. The skill scans `references/committees/` to build the selection menu; adding a committee is just adding a folder. There is no hard-coded roster. Two kinds:
    - **Real-people** committees (e.g. `real-committee`, or a field-luminaries panel the user assembles) — actual named people. Profiles are neutral ground-truth facts only (role, research focus, key themes, representative work); the *lens and questioning style* are the agent's read and live in a prep companion (or are inferred from the profile when none exists), never in the profile. A profile may sit beside a same-stem BibTeX companion (`profiles/<member>.bib` — e.g. the member's publications since 2020, exported from a reference manager): when present, load it with the profile as the authoritative record of that member's recent work. Pair by stem, tolerantly: a `.bib` whose stem matches no profile exactly still belongs to the nearest-named member (exports often differ by a middle initial) — pair it and say so; when two profiles are near-named and the match is ambiguous, ask rather than guess (a mis-pair attributes one member's publications to another). These files routinely exceed a whole-file read window (thousands of lines): extract with a filter — e.g. grep the title/booktitle/journal/year lines — rather than reading the file whole, keep each entry's title, venue, and year bound to its own entry (never cross-wire fields between entries), and treat any partial read as an incomplete record, not the member's full publication list. User-maintained — do not rewrite a profile or a `.bib` without being asked; propose even purely factual fixes (a wrong venue, a mistyped title) rather than applying them.
    - **Fictional-roles** committees (`proxy-panel`, `cognitive-council`, `specialist-council`) — exam roles, cognitive lenses, or review criteria, not real people. Here the profile *is* the role definition (its job, what it probes, its reasoning method), because nothing is being inferred about a real person.
- **Event prep companions** live inside the committee folder, named by event (e.g. `references/committees/real-committee/<event>-prep.md`). An *event* is a short slug naming one defence occasion — e.g. `candidacy`, or a thesis slug — and it also names the report file. The prep companion holds the cross-cutting design pressure points, the per-member likely questions, and each member's questioning style (the profiles give the lens, the prep file gives the style and sample questions). Use it as the question seed bank, then generate fresh questions grounded in the document and adapt to the candidate's answers. The prep file's predicted and inferred sections are the agent's derived read and agent-refinable; the profiles are ground truth — when the two disagree about a member, trust the profile and refine the prep file. One carve-out: any dated captured record of a real encounter folded into a prep file (an "at the real exam" section) is ground truth like `asked-questions.md` — refine predictions around it, never rewrite the record itself. Fictional committees have no prep companion — they seed from their source document instead (`mock-committee-best-practices.md` for the proxy panel; `llm-council-panel.md` for the two councils), named in each manifest's "question source". A real-people committee folder may also carry an `asked-questions.md` — the questions its members actually asked at past real exams, one dated section per encounter, recorded from the user's own account. When present, load it with the committee for any event: a question a member actually asked is ground truth and outranks the prep file's predictions for calibrating register, difficulty, and coverage, and its recorded drill lessons shape the session design. Append to it only from the user's account of a real encounter; never invent or embellish an entry, and state capture limits explicitly — a member whose questions the notes did not capture is recorded as uncaptured, never as having asked nothing.
- **Ground every question in reality.** A question rests on what the document actually claims and — for real-people committees — on a member's real profile. Never invent a real member's paper, position, or stance, never attribute a false premise to the proposal, and never invent a citation. If a profile is thin or a member's expertise is unrelated to the topic, question from their general examiner standards (rigour, statistics, motivation, scope) rather than fabricating specifics. (Example error to avoid: labelling a member with a subfield they do not actually work in.) For fictional committees the same discipline holds in a different form: the seats are roles, so never pin a real paper or named stance on them — they press from the role's job, its reasoning method or review criterion, and what the document actually says. Grounding also does not confine questioning to the document: real examiners bring published work the document never cites and ask how the proposal relates, so drills must too. Voice a specific named work, system, or study only when it comes from a grounded well — the member's `.bib`, their profile, the prep file, or the defended document's own reference list; a work recalled only from model memory is never voiced as a specific citation (model confidence is not evidence a work exists). Otherwise use the area-level pointer, phrased the way examiners actually recall literature ("the control community has decades of work on stability guarantees — how does your method relate to that?"), or name the recalled work with an explicit flag that the candidate must verify it before relying on it. A member's `.bib` companion is the safest well for that member's own-work probes. An honest area-level pointer is realistic; a fabricated citation is never acceptable.
- **Adversarial in service of prep.** Challenge hard; steelman the design, then attack the seam. On the real committee, supervisors are backstops in register — supportive framing and plan questions rather than attacks on the seams — but not silent and not exempt from a full questioning turn each round (`references/real-exam-calibration.md`: every member questions, supervisors included; a silent supervisor seat rehearses a narrower room), and where a committee's `asked-questions.md` shows a supervisor pressed hard, that outranks the backstop default. Play each member as the profile and prep file say; even the member best aligned with the work may still press hard on rigour, and arms-length examiners press hardest. Fictional committees have no supervisors — every seat presses, calibrated by its role (the Contrarian and the near-field expert hardest; a chair/chairman frames and synthesizes rather than attacks). Guard against your own drift: one model role-plays the examiners and grades their answers, so the pull is toward agreement — waving a wobbly answer through, warming the verdict to be helpful. This same-model softening (the sycophancy failure `references/llm-council-panel.md` names) is present in every committee here, the real one included, not only the fictional councils. Pitch questions and the verdict to the real exam's intensity for this event — the bar the profiles, prep, and `references/real-exam-calibration.md` warrant — resisting the drift below it. Calibrate to that bar, not past it: a drill harsher than the real room mis-teaches too, so reveal failure points while staying safe enough to permit mistakes (`references/mock-committee-best-practices.md`, Bottom Line), never demoralize. A mock the candidate passes but the real room would fail is the failure this skill exists to prevent.
- **Resuming prior sessions.** Before a drill, the skill scans `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/` for earlier reports on the same event and builds a carry-forward set — the weak spots, untested seams, and to-dos the last session left open. When a carry-forward set exists, the session offers to resume and retarget those open seams rather than start cold, re-testing each against the current document to see whether it has been closed; a fresh run stays available, and a first session on an event has nothing to resume. See `references/resume.md`.
- **Match the user's spelling convention** in agent-authored prose (this vault: Canadian spelling); `a-archive/style/ai-writing-tells.md` (when the vault carries it) governs the prose. The committee's spoken questions are role-play, not wiki prose, but still avoid the AI-writing tells.

## When To Invoke

Any request to practise, rehearse, or be grilled for a candidacy or thesis defence; to run committee questions; or to be mock-examined by the committee. Also any request to consolidate the prior sessions for an event into one standing view — a running weak-spot ledger of where the candidate stands across runs (see *Modes → Cross-session consolidation*).

## When Not To Invoke

- Building wiki pages from a source. Use `ingest`.
- Answering a research question from the wiki. Use `query`.
- A general chat about the proposal's content, or help drafting it — handle as ordinary conversation; this skill is the live exam drill, not co-writing.

## Procedure

```text
Defence Progress:
- [ ] Step 1: Load mock-defence-memory.md and multi-skill-memory.md
- [ ] Step 2: Identify the document and event; read the document (confirm full-text coverage)
- [ ] Step 3: Scan prior sessions on this event; build the resume/retarget set (or branch to consolidation mode if that was the request)
- [ ] Step 4: Select the committee (scan references/committees/, ask the user), then load it
- [ ] Step 5: Confirm the rest of scope (mode, members, focus, detail level, resume vs fresh)
- [ ] Step 6: Run the session (interactive drill or question bank), retargeting open seams when resuming
- [ ] Step 7: Save the session report — and, for a drill, the verbatim transcript — to .claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/
- [ ] Step 8: Append a memory entry on any user-confirmed correction
```

1. **Load orientation.** Read `.claude/skills/mock-defence/mock-defence-memory.md` and `.claude/skills/multi-skill/multi-skill-memory.md` (when present — a standalone copy of this folder may lack the multi-skill file). Apply prior corrections about framing and member lenses before questioning.

2. **Identify the document and event, then read it.** Default to the document and event named in `references/defaults.md`; otherwise the user names them. Read the document fully — especially the proposal chapter, hypotheses, and evaluation — then confirm coverage concretely: note the document's page count, read through to the final page (reference list, appendices, or a clear end), and verify no chunk returned empty or image-only. State the page range actually read in the report's Scope. If the read stops short of the end or any chunk failed to extract, treat it as the unreadable-document edge case (stop and flag), not a complete read. For a document too large to hold verbatim across a long drill (a full thesis), read it fully here to build the question set and a section map, then re-open specific sections during the drill rather than relying on the whole text staying in context. If the document can't be found, ask which file. While reading, derive the document's own pressure points — its load-bearing assumptions, the confounds and alternative explanations its design invites, and the claims most exposed to a counter — and carry them into the question set; these document-grounded seams are what a hard drill presses when there is no prep file and are the fictional panels' primary hard material, so without them a no-prep or fictional drill leans on generic questions and cannot be hard by default.

3. **Scan prior sessions; build the resume/retarget set.** Following `references/resume.md`, scan `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/` for earlier reports on this event (skip the `*-transcript.md` companions). Extract the carry-forward set — the open weak spots, the untested or skipped seams, the room-verdict conditions, and the unfinished to-dos the last session left open — netting out anything a later report marked closed. Prefer a prior consolidation ledger (a `mode: synthesis` report, skill-generated by consolidation mode) as the best single summary when one exists. State the set plainly before committee selection: how many open seams carry forward, from which reports, and which cuts deepest. If no report exists for this event, there is nothing to resume — run a fresh drill, the unchanged default. If the request is a consolidation rather than a drill, branch here to the consolidation mode (see *Modes → Cross-session consolidation*): fold the scanned reports into the standing ledger and stop, skipping committee selection, scope, and the drill (Steps 4–6).

4. **Select the committee, then load it.** Scan `references/committees/` and read each `committee.md` manifest to build the menu (display name, kind, one-line summary). Use `AskUserQuestion` to let the user pick which committee convenes — their real committee, any panel they have assembled (e.g. field luminaries), or a fictional panel (proxy panel, cognitive council, specialist council). Recommend `real-committee` for genuine exam prep and order it first; name the fictional panels as alternates for a different angle. Then load the chosen committee: every profile in its `profiles/` (ignore a leading-underscore `_TEMPLATE.md`) along with any `.bib` companions (paired per Conventions), and its question seed — for a real-people committee the matching prep companion (`<event>-prep.md`) if present; for a fictional committee the source document named in its manifest's "question source" (`mock-committee-best-practices.md` or `llm-council-panel.md`). For a real-people committee, also load `asked-questions.md` when the folder has one — the ground-truth record of what this committee actually asked at past real exams (see Conventions); it applies to any event, not only the one it was recorded at. If a real-people committee has no prep for the event, proceed from the document and profiles alone and say so. If the chosen committee's `profiles/` is empty (e.g. a just-created luminaries panel), say so and offer another committee.

5. **Confirm the rest of scope.** Use `AskUserQuestion` — one decision per call — to settle: mode (interactive drill — default — or written question bank); which members of the chosen committee are at the table (all, or a subset); any focus (a chapter, a known-weak area, a single member); and the detail level — `high-level` (substantive design, contribution, framing, evaluation soundness), `nit-pick` (the granular pass: exact wording, notation, citation and figure/table details, small inconsistencies), or `mixed` (both, the default and the closest match to a real exam). Recommend `mixed` and order it first; name `nit-pick` as the register to pick when the user wants to rehearse against a committee that grills on small points. **When Step 3 found a carry-forward set, also ask whether to resume and retarget the open seams (recommended default — order it first) or run a fresh full drill** (the pick when the proposal was substantially rewritten or the user wants breadth); with no carry-forward set, skip this question and run fresh. Keep it to the genuine choices; don't over-ask.

6. **Run the session.** See *Modes* below. Two general backstops: `references/mock-committee-best-practices.md` (read its drill-facing sections — structure, candidacy-vs-final design, session protocol and questioning rules, the core question bank and feedback rubric, and the common pitfalls; the recruitment templates, the recruit-the-panel timeline, and the reference list are human-panel logistics an AI drill does not need) and `references/llm-council-panel.md` (the council role sets, reasoning-method mapping, and chair-synthesis structure). For a real-people committee, its profiles and prep stay primary and the best-practices file fills gaps and calibrates the closing verdict — it does not override *these* examiners. For a fictional committee, its source document *is* the primary seed (proxy panel → mock-committee-best-practices; councils → llm-council-panel), with the other backstop drawn on for the verdict rubric and candidacy-vs-final calibration.

7. **Save the session report** to `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/mock-defence-YYYY-MM-DD-HHMM-{event}.md`. Obtain the timestamp once at write time with `date '+%Y-%m-%d-%H%M'` — prefixed `TZ='<timezone>'` when `references/defaults.md` pins one, so report names sort consistently across machines — and reuse it for both files. Create `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/` if missing. Write a new file every run — never overwrite a prior session. Follow the skeleton in `references/report-format.md`: the session scope (including the detail level), the questions asked with a short verdict on how each answer held and — for each answer that did not hold — the coaching note on how to strengthen it (the live `Coach:` addendum, preserved), the surfaced weak spots, the coverage section (which document sections, registers, and members were tested versus left untested), a closing room-level verdict (would the room pass / pass-with-conditions / not-yet, calibrated to the event), and the reading/tightening to-dos. **When the session resumed**, also record the continuity (per `references/resume.md`): the prior reports built on in the `resumes:` frontmatter and Scope, and each carried-forward seam's outcome (closed / improved / still open) in the `Carried-forward weak spots (retargeted)` section — so the `Weak spots` section is this session's still-open set, the hand-off the next session resumes from. Attribute questions by member name, but do not transcribe profile content into the report (the privacy rule, `references/report-format.md`) — it records the questions and the candidate's answers, not the real examiners' personalities. **For an interactive drill, also write the verbatim transcript** to `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/mock-defence-YYYY-MM-DD-HHMM-{event}-transcript.md` (same timestamp and event) — it exists so the candidate can review their exact phrasing afterward, which the summarized report deliberately omits — per the Transcript skeleton in `references/report-format.md`: the full turn-by-turn record in order — questions as voiced, the candidate's answers verbatim, every counter and follow-up, the in-role verdicts, and any out-of-role `Coach:`/`pause` turns where they occurred. Write it from the actual conversation; never reconstruct turns that did not happen. The question-bank mode has no dialogue and writes no transcript.

8. **Capture corrections.** If the user corrects a member's lens, a factual premise, or how the drill should run, append an entry to `mock-defence-memory.md` (newest on top). Don't rewrite existing entries.

## Modes

**Interactive drill (default).** A live, multi-turn oral-exam rehearsal:

- At the start, tell the candidate the control words they can use (listed below), then begin.
- Ask one question at a time, then stop and wait for the candidate's reply. Never wall them with several questions, and never answer your own question — the candidate must produce the answer.
- Keep the answer key behind the question, and evaluate only what the candidate actually says. You have read the prep file, which carries each member's likely questions and the seam to probe; use it to choose where to press — never to reveal, hint at, or grade against a model answer during questioning. Before voicing a question, ground it: confirm its premise against the document you read, and for a real-people seat confirm any attributed paper, finding, or stance against that member's loaded profile, prep file, or `.bib` companion — if it is in none of them, drop the attribution and press from general examiner standards. Voice a question drawn from a member's inferred stance or captured past behaviour as a neutral probe on its topic, never as that member's asserted position — what is voiced is what the transcript will faithfully record. Re-check a prep-file specific (a number, a condition count, a quoted stance) against the live document before using it, since the prep file may be stale; keep the document in context across the drill so questions stay tied to the text, not memory — and when a late question rests on a document specific (a number, a quoted claim, a section) that may have scrolled out of a long drill's context, re-read that section (`pages:`) before voicing it rather than trusting recollection. Compare against a model answer only when stepping out of role: when the candidate calls `pause` to debrief, in the coaching addendum after a weak answer (below), in the closing verdict, or — when resuming — in the out-of-role closed / improved / still open call on each carried-forward seam. A resumed carry-forward set is a second answer key: the prior report's `How to strengthen` notes and room-verdict conditions stay behind the question exactly as the prep file's model answers do (see `references/resume.md`). A live `Coach:` note is likewise a second answer key within the session (see the coaching addendum below): do not re-voice it, hint at it, or let a later round grade the candidate's recital of it as recall.
- Attribute each question to a member by name or role, opening with the tag. For a real-people committee that is the member name (the surname, e.g. `Khan:`); fix the member's *lens* from their profile (research focus, key themes), take their *questioning style and sample questions* from the prep file, and where neither gives a style, question from that member's general examiner standards. For a fictional committee that is the role (e.g. `Contrarian:`, `Near-field expert:`, `Methods reviewer:`); fix the role's lens from its profile, and take its reasoning method or review criterion from the backstop document. A chair/chairman seat frames and synthesizes rather than interrogating — give it the opening framing question and the closing verdict, not the hard technical follow-ups.
- Push back on each answer — follow-ups, "but you conceded X", requests to be precise. An answer *holds* when it states the claim, its mechanism or evidence, and survives one genuine counter (a genuine counter states the tension and asks the candidate to resolve it, never supplying the resolution); press at most two or three follow-ups, then move on. The verdict is earned, not granted: give a one-line verdict that names plainly where an answer would not satisfy the room — a weak answer that survives the follow-up cap is logged as a weak spot, not pressed forever. Calibrate the bar to the event: a candidacy probes whether the plan is sound; a thesis probes whether the work is done. When you are genuinely unsure whether the candidate's answer cleared the counter, round down — resolve the uncertainty against them, since it is theirs to close and the model's own helpfulness is the adversary here. Because that round-down fires only on felt uncertainty, while same-model softening presents as confidence rather than doubt, put a forcing function on every borderline hold: before logging a borderline answer as *holds*, state in one clause the case that it *wobbles*, and log *holds* only if that case genuinely fails — turning the per-turn verdict from a disposition into an articulation, so leniency cannot ride silently the way it can slip the closing verdict's downstream check. This applies only to a borderline candidate answer, never to your own doubt about a question: an examiner unsure its objection is sound checks it against the document and drops it if unfounded, rather than marking a sound answer a wobble. The closing room verdict follows the same discipline (see `references/report-format.md`): derive it from the logged weak spots — an open load-bearing seam caps it below pass, and one decisive unresolved objection outweighs several holds — and state the case for the next-harsher verdict before landing rather than defaulting to a pass.
- **Detail level governs which register the questions sit at** (set in Step 5, switchable mid-drill). `high-level` keeps questions on substantive design, contribution, framing, and evaluation soundness, and deliberately skips wording, notation, and citation-format quibbles. `nit-pick` runs the granular pass — exact wording, notation, citation and figure/table details, small inconsistencies — the register to drill when rehearsing against a committee that grills on small points. `mixed` (default) does both: lead with the substantive questions, then close a topic with the small points a picky examiner would still raise. Detail level is independent of `harder`/`easier`, which sets difficulty within whatever register is active — a question can be high-level-and-hard or nit-pick-and-gentle.
- **Coaching addendum after a weak answer (opt-in).** By default this drill is a pure no-coaching mock, matching the protocol in `references/mock-committee-best-practices.md`; the candidate turns coaching on with `coach on` for a workshop-style teaching run — realism kept first and the coaching second — and returns to pure realism with `coach off`. Section 6 of that file makes no-inline-coaching the default (which is why coaching is off unless asked for) but carves an explicit exception for a session that is explicitly a workshop, and `coach on` is that opt-in; §6's attached concern — that inline coaching prevents the candidate from practising recovery — is met even in that mode by letting the member finish in role first and by the contamination guard below, so recovery is still practised. When coaching is on, let the member finish in role first (the counter, any follow-ups, and the one-line verdict); then, only when the answer did not hold — it was illogical, dodged the question, asserted a claim with no mechanism or evidence, or conceded the seam — step out of role under a plain `Coach:` tag and say briefly how to make the argument stronger: what the room needed to hear, the missing mechanism, evidence, or scope qualifier, and which stage of the answer scaffold in `references/mock-committee-best-practices.md` (pause → clarify → answer → support → qualify → close) the answer skipped — name the stage to rebuild, the form, not the content that fills it, so the fix stays a portable skill and does not hand over the answer the room is still testing. Keep it to two or three sentences — a pointer toward a stronger answer, not the full model answer recited. Then drop back in role and immediately re-ask that same seam once, so the candidate deploys the fix under pressure now rather than recognizing it in a later round: this coached re-attempt is deliberate practice, judged as its own thing — did the rebuilt answer land, or is it still shaky with the fix in hand — and logged as such, but never recorded as the seam closed, since the candidate answered with the coach's pointer in view. A `Coach:` note and its re-attempt are a stored answer for the rest of this session, the within-session twin of the resumed carry-forward set: that immediate re-ask is the only place a coached seam is re-tested inside the drill — do not re-quiz it again in a later round and score a recited rebuild as a fresh pass (that grades recognition as recall); its real cold re-test is a later session, where the note sits behind the question like any model answer. An answer that holds gets no addendum and no re-attempt; the in-role "holds" verdict is the whole turn.
- **Cover the four registers one real candidacy exam used** (distilled into `references/real-exam-calibration.md`; a committee's own `asked-questions.md`, when present, outranks them): fundamentals spot-checks — early formal questions from the field's shared canon, which test the candidate rather than the document and do not track the asking member's own specialty; uncited-literature probes — at least one per arms-length examiner, under the grounding convention's honesty constraint; breadth beyond the document — the testbeds, representations, and framings the candidate did not choose, and what is genuinely specific to this work versus the adjacent field's prior art; and the logistics close — plan, timeline, venues, readiness, formation — because that exam ended on the candidate, not the document. Some turns are position statements the candidate must engage ("[the studied mechanism] has a cost — less of it is better"), not questions. The registers are a strong default, not a universal: they are n=1 and candidacy-shaped, so reweight them by event and field — a thesis drill lightens the fundamentals register and reshapes the logistics close around the completed work's contribution and future (per `references/mock-committee-best-practices.md` section 3), and a field or format whose real exams run differently (a closed viva, a non-experimental discipline) calibrates against its own format first. A drill that skips a register that fits the event rehearses a narrower exam than the real one.
- Escalate: fundamentals spot-checks (cold, from the field's shared canon — the real room's actual opening per `references/real-exam-calibration.md`, not a gentle warm-up; the chair's opening framing question is the only non-adversarial entry) → the cross-cutting design pressure points → each member's sharpest questions → a second round per member, going to fresh or harder ground rather than re-quizzing a seam a `Coach:` note just rebuilt (re-quizzing a coached seam grades recognition, not recall; real committees also run the table more than once rather than retiring a member after one pass, and the logistics register often rides these second-round tails) → a synthesis question ("the single cleanest contribution") → the logistics close (whatever formation-and-plan ground the second round left uncovered) → the closing room verdict. **When resuming** (see `references/resume.md`), the opening changes: instead of opening cold on fundamentals, re-probe the deepest carried-forward seam with a question that names the topic but not the prior verdict, re-testing each open seam against the current document (closed / improved / still open) and pressing harder on any that survived a prior drill — then move to fresh ground, the synthesis question, and the same logistics-close-and-verdict ending. The drill spans many turns. Once the first question is asked (Step 6), the drill is live: on every later turn treat the candidate's message as an answer or a control word, and continue the drill already in progress — do not reload memory, re-scan prior sessions, re-select the committee, or re-confirm scope mid-drill. It ends after the logistics close and the closing verdict — the synthesis question is not the finish — or when the candidate says `stop`/`done`; if the candidate seems to be winding down without saying so, ask once whether to close before writing. Write the report and transcript (Step 7) only after the drill has ended — one report and one transcript per session.
- Control words: `pause` (step out of role to debrief — one place model answers are shared), `skip` (move on; the skipped question is logged as an untested weak spot), `switch to <member>`, `harder` / `easier`, `big picture` (or `high-level`) / `nitpick` (or `nit-pick`) / `mixed` (set the detail level on the fly — high-level only, granular only, or both; the switch words and the Step 5 detail-level names are interchangeable), `coach off` / `coach on` (turn the coaching addendum and its immediate re-attempt after weak answers off or on; off by default — a pure-realism mock unless coaching is turned on), `stop` / `done` (end and write the report and transcript).
- Track weak spots and coverage as they appear, for the report: keep a running tally — which document sections and design pressure points have been pressed, which of the four registers have fired, and which seated members have questioned — and re-state it compactly every several turns so it survives a long drill's context loss. Before the logistics close, reconcile that tally against the Step 2 section map, the four registers, and the roster of seated members: press, or explicitly log as untested, every unreached section, register, and member, so the drill does not reach the verdict leaving them silently unexamined.

Worked beats (illustrative shape, not a script):

- In-character question (open with the member's named-seat tag): `Khan: You attribute the improvement to your scheduling policy, but you also doubled the cache between versions — which change produced the gain?`
- Push-back (a different seat — here a fictional reviewer — calling back the candidate's own earlier concession): `Methods reviewer: You earlier conceded the two versions differ in more than the policy — yet your contribution statement credits the policy alone. Name the comparison that isolates it, or the claim doesn't hold.`
- Verdict: `Holds on motivation; the evaluation is the soft spot — no run isolates the policy from the cache change, so this wobbles.`
- Coaching addendum (out of role, fired only because the answer above did not hold): `Coach: You gave the claim but skipped support and qualify — the room needed the isolating run and its number, then the scope it holds in. Rebuild on the scaffold: answer (the claim) → support (the run where only the policy differs, and its number) → qualify (that scope). Name the skipped stage, don't recite the fix.`
- Immediate re-attempt (back in role, same seam, so the candidate deploys the fix under pressure — logged as coached practice, not the seam closed): `Khan: Then give it to me now — name the single run where only the scheduling policy differs, and tell me what it showed.`

**Question bank.** No live drill: produce a written set of likely questions per member, grounded in the document, the profile, and — when the committee carries one — the `asked-questions.md` ground truth, covering the same four registers as the drill (fundamentals, uncited literature, breadth, the logistics close). Each entry carries the member, the question, the strongest answer, the seam a committee would probe, and a locator into the proposal (and the profile line, where it invokes a member's own work). Use the template in `references/report-format.md`. Save to the same report path; useful for solo review between drills.

**Cross-session consolidation (the standing ledger).** No committee and no drill: fold every session report for the event into one living ledger of where the candidate stands. Run Steps 1–3 (memory, the document and event, the prior-session scan), then write or refresh the ledger — the fold-and-recompute logic is in `references/resume.md` ("Consolidation mode"), the skeleton in `references/report-format.md` — skipping committee selection (Step 4), scope (Step 5), and the drill (Step 6). The ledger is one stable file per event (`mock-defence-{event}-ledger.md`, `mode: synthesis`), refreshed in place after each later run, because a seam that was a weakness may now be fixed and new weaknesses surface each session — so every refresh recomputes the open set rather than freezing it. Offer it as an alternative when the Step 3 scan finds prior reports, or run it directly when the user asks to consolidate or summarize where they stand across sessions.

## Reference Files

- `references/defaults.md` — user-maintained: the default document under defence and its event slug (Step 2).
- `references/committees/<name>/committee.md` — short manifest per committee (one line per field: display name, kind, summary, question source); read to build the selection menu (Step 4).
- `references/committees/<name>/profiles/*.md` — one profile per member or role. Real-people committees carry neutral facts (the prep file holds the lens/style); fictional committees carry the role definition itself (Steps 4, 6). A leading-underscore file (e.g. `_TEMPLATE.md`) is a scaffold, not a member. A same-stem `<member>.bib` beside a real-people profile is that member's publication record, loaded with the profile (Steps 4, 6).
- `references/committees/<name>/<event>-prep.md` (real-people committees) — the event's design pressure points, per-member likely questions, and questioning style; the question seed bank (Steps 4, 6).
- `references/committees/<name>/asked-questions.md` — optional, real-people committees only: the questions the committee actually asked at past real exams, dated per encounter. Ground truth that outranks prep-file predictions and carries the drill lessons; loaded for any event (Steps 4, 6).
- `references/real-exam-calibration.md` — how one real candidacy exam ran, names and proposal specifics abstracted (the empirical-science exam shape remains — its intro says how to adapt): the four registers with question archetypes, the seat patterns, and the drill lessons. - `references/resume.md` — how to scan prior session reports for this event, build the carry-forward set of open weak spots, offer resume vs fresh, retarget the open seams in the drill, record the continuity, and (consolidation mode) fold the sessions into a refreshed standing ledger (Steps 3, 5, 6, 7).
- `references/report-format.md` — the session-report, question-bank, consolidation-ledger, and verbatim-transcript skeletons, including the privacy rule, the detail-level field, and the resume fields (Step 7).
- `references/mock-committee-best-practices.md` — general, member-agnostic guidance on mock-committee structure, session protocol, the core question bank, the feedback rubric, and candidacy-vs-final-defence design; the proxy panel's primary seed and the backstop drawn on where any committee's prep is thin, plus the calibration for the closing verdict (Step 6); its recruitment templates, panel-recruiting timeline, and reference list are human-panel material an AI drill can skip.

## Edge Cases

- **No committees at all** (`references/committees/` empty or missing). Run a generic committee from the proxy-panel roles (chair, near-field expert, broader-field examiner, recent passer) and tell the user that adding committees under `references/committees/` makes the drill far more realistic.
- **No `references/defaults.md`, or it names no document.** Ask which file to defend and which event slug to use, and offer to write the defaults file so later runs skip the question.
- **The chosen committee's `profiles/` is empty, missing, or holds only ignored files** (e.g. a just-created luminaries panel, or a lone `_TEMPLATE.md`). Say so and offer another committee; do not invent members to fill it. A `committee.md` with no `kind:` field is flagged and treated conservatively as real-people (neutral facts only, no attributed stances) until the user sets it — `kind:` routes the grounding discipline, so it is never guessed.
- **No prep companion for the event** (real-people committee). Generate questions from the document and profiles directly, seeding from the core question bank in `references/mock-committee-best-practices.md` and from the document's own pressure points derived in Step 2 (its load-bearing assumptions, confounds, and most-exposed claims), so the drill has document-specific hard seams rather than only the generic bank; flag that there's no curated seed bank yet for this event.
- **No prior sessions on this event, or the proposal was rewritten since.** With no earlier report for the event, there is nothing to resume — run a fresh drill (the unchanged default). When prior reports exist but defended a different document path, or the proposal has been substantially rewritten, treat the carry-forward set as advisory: confirm each seam against the current text before raising it, and offer the fresh-run option. See `references/resume.md` for the full resume edge cases.
- **A profile is thin, or the member's field is unrelated to the topic.** Question from that member's general examiner standards (rigour, statistics, motivation, external validity), never from invented specifics.
- **The document can't be read** (partial extract, image-only, OCR-garbled). Stop and flag it; don't run a drill from a half-read proposal.
- **The drill is interrupted before it ends** (window closed, context lost). Nothing is on disk yet — the report and transcript are written only at Step 7 — so an interrupted drill leaves no report and the Step 3 scan cannot resume it. Start fresh; if the scrollback is still available, the user may ask to reconstruct a report from it before starting.
- **The user wants to defend a different document** (thesis, a paper talk). Use it, load a matching prep file if one exists, otherwise proceed from document and profiles.
- **One context, several members.** A single context role-playing several examiners tends to blur their voices together. Re-ground each question in that member's own profile (or role) to keep them distinct, and treat perfect voice-separation as a known limit, not a guarantee. Fictional roles separate more cleanly than real members because each is pinned to a distinct reasoning method or review criterion.
- **The observer seat (proxy panel) in a text drill.** It is logistics-only and has little to do without a live presentation; keep it out of the questioning rotation and fold its function into the report's delivery notes (see its profile).

## Limits

- **Prep-only and wiki-orthogonal.** Writes only to `.claude/skills/mock-defence/outputs/`; touches no `1-wiki/` bookkeeping (`log.md`, `index.md`, `hot.md`), so CLAUDE.md's wiki-scoped "log every operation" rule does not apply.
- Never modify `0-raw/`.
- **Never fabricate** a real committee member's research, papers, positions, or a false premise about the document; ground every question, or stay general. Never invent a citation. The same rule binds fictional committees in a different form: their seats are roles, not people, so never pin a real paper or named stance on them — they press from the role's job, its reasoning method or review criterion, and the document.
- **A fictional panel is the weakest predictor of the real exam.** Same-model role-play shares blind spots and is lower-confidence than a real near-field expert on field-specific critique (see `references/llm-council-panel.md` failure-mode caveats). Use fictional panels to harden reasoning, communication, and the contribution statement; pair them with the real committee or proxy panel for technical depth, and say so if the user over-trusts a fictional drill. Two distinct same-model failures ride here: domain blind-spots (worse for a fictional panel, and what pairing with the real committee or proxy panel cures) and sycophantic softening — the drift toward easy questions and generous verdicts — which is present in every committee including the real one and is not cured by pairing (see the anti-softening guard in *Adversarial in service of prep*). That anti-softening guard has a ceiling the domain-competence ceiling below does not cover: it is self-applied by the same pass that is softening, so it dampens the drift but cannot be trusted to remove it — sharpest on the real-committee drill, whose only structural anti-consensus guard (preserved dissent) is council-only. A distinct failure compounds it: on the candidate's own contribution, where a same-model examiner knows *less* than the candidate, the drift is a false pass by *ignorance*, so no felt uncertainty fires the round-down and the false-fail guard retires exactly the niche objections a real expert would press. Pairing cannot cure this on the real drill (there is no more-expert panel to pair with), so treat the drill's nod on your core contribution as its least trustworthy verdict and get a human near-field reader for that part.
- **The examiner can be wrong, not only the candidate.** A same-model examiner may mark a correct answer a wobble on a hallucinated objection or a false premise, worst in the candidate's own niche where the model's grasp is thinnest. A surprising or harsh verdict is a cue to check the examiner's premise against the document, not to trust it as ground truth — the round-down-when-unsure rule (Modes) resolves the candidate's borderline answers against them, never the model's doubt about its own objection. This caps how far any honesty guard can go: the drill's grading is only as reliable as the model's command of the field.
- Role-play is a rehearsal aid, not a prediction of the real exam — say so if the user treats a drilled answer as guaranteed. A typed drill also rehearses arguments, not spoken delivery — composure, interruptions, and pacing go unpractised — and a simulated examiner's specific objections are informed guesses the real room will diverge from.
- **The machinery stays field-neutral.** SKILL.md, README.md, and the top-level `references/*.md` carry no user-specific content — no real names, institutions, fields, documents, or *identifying* exam specifics; every illustrative example is invented (the one exception, `references/real-exam-calibration.md`, keeps the field-generalized shape of one real exam — its four registers and seat patterns — with names, institution, field, and proposal abstracted out). Personal data lives only in the user-owned files (`references/committees/`, `references/defaults.md`, and the memory journal). Keep that boundary when editing the skill.
- **Real-people** committee profiles (e.g. `real-committee`, a user-assembled luminaries panel) describe real people. Keep them in this prep context; don't reproduce them elsewhere — including the saved report and transcript, which name questions by member but not their personal characterizations (see `references/report-format.md`). They are the user's to maintain — don't rewrite a profile or a `.bib` without being asked. (This vault also permission-locks `real-committee/profiles/` against edits.) Fictional-role profiles describe no one and carry no such privacy constraint.
- Don't update the defended document, the wiki, or the user's other memory files; corrections go only to `mock-defence-memory.md`.
